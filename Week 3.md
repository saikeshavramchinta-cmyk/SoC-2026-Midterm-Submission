## Catching the Breakout
### What's driving the price?
1. Momentum trading relies on the core philosophy that assets exhibiting strong performance in the recent past will continue that trajectory in the short term.
2. We want to programmatically identify **breakouts**: moments when the price aggressively moves outside a defined support or resistance level, typically accompanied by a surge in volume.

### Building the Indicators
We need mathematical ways to quantify this momentum, some popular indicators include Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD), Relative Strength Index (RSI), Rate of Change (ROC), and many others.

For this week, we will focus on implementing MACD and RSI from scratch.


**Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD):** 
A trend-following momentum indicator showing the relationship between two exponential moving averages (EMAs). It is calculated by subtracting the 26-period EMA from the 12-period EMA:

$$MACD = EMA_{12} - EMA_{26}$$

To build the MACD, we first need to understand the **Exponential Moving Average (EMA)**. Unlike a simple moving average (SMA) which gives equal weight to all days, the EMA places a greater weight and significance on the most recent data points, making it more responsive to new information. 

The formula for an EMA at time $t$ is:

$$EMA_t = \left( Price_t \times \frac{2}{N + 1} \right) + \left( EMA_{t-1} \times \left( 1 - \frac{2}{N + 1} \right) \right)$$

Where $N$ is the number of lookback periods.

The MACD line is calculated by subtracting the long-term EMA from the short-term EMA (traditionally 26-period and 12-period):
$$MACD_t = EMA_{12}(t) - EMA_{26}(t)$$

To generate a signal, we calculate a 9-period EMA of the MACD line itself, called the **Signal Line**. 
* **Buy Signal:** When the MACD crosses *above* the Signal Line.
* **Sell Signal:** When the MACD crosses *below* the Signal Line.

**Relative Strength Index (RSI)**
RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the magnitude of recent gains relative to recent losses.

First, calculate the average gain and average loss over a lookback window (traditionally 14 periods):

$$RS = \frac{\text{Average Gain}}{\text{Average Loss}}$$

The RSI is then:

$$RSI = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + RS}$$

The indicator ranges between 0 and 100.

Common interpretations are:
* RSI > 70 → Overbought
* RSI < 30 → Oversold

For our purposes, RSI will act as an additional confirmation signal alongside MACD.

## Filtering the Noise 

Not every signal generated by our indicators is a true breakout. Financial data is notoriously noisy. If we trade on every minor crossover, transaction costs and false starts (fakeouts) will drain the portfolio.

### The Volume Filter

We can write a filter to ensure that any MACD signal is accompanied by a mathematically significant surge in volume. We do this by comparing the current period's volume to a rolling Simple Moving Average (SMA) of the volume over the last $W$ periods. A breakout without a corresponding increase in trading volume is highly suspect. If price is moving aggressively but volume remains low, there may not be enough market participation to sustain the move.

We calculate a rolling Simple Moving Average (SMA) of volume:

$$SMA_t^{Vol} = \frac{1}{W} \sum_{i=0}^{W-1} Volume_{t-i}$$

where $W$ is the lookback window.

We then define a threshold multiplier $c$:

$$Volume_t > c \times SMA_t^{Vol}$$

A signal is only considered valid if the current volume exceeds this threshold.

For example, if a MACD crossover occurs but volume fails this condition, the signal should be ignored.

## What is MACD?
MACD (Moving Average Convergence/Divergence) is a popular tool traders use to figure out the momentum of a stock's price. Basically, it helps we see if a trend is getting stronger or weaker, which can help we decide the best time to buy or sell.
**The Three Main Pieces of MACD**
Instead of looking just at the daily price, MACD looks at averages over time. It has three main parts we'll see on a trading chart:
## The MACD Line:
This is the heart of the tool. It compares short-term price momentum against long-term price momentum.
## The Signal Line:
Think of this as the "trigger" line. It's a slightly smoothed-out, slower version of the MACD line.
### The Histogram:
A bar chart that simply shows the distance between the MACD Line and the Signal Line, making it easy to see who is winning the tug-of-war.
## The Math Behind It:
If we are curious how it's calculated behind the scenes, it simply subtracts a longer-term average (26 periods) from a shorter-term average (12 periods).
### $$MACD=EMA_{12}-EMA_{26}$$
(EMA stands for Exponential Moving Average, which is just a fancy way of saying it cares more about recent prices than older ones).How Traders Read the SignalsTraders look at how these lines interact to make decisions. Here are the three main things they watch for:
### Crossovers (The most common signal):
**Buy Signal (Bullish)**: The MACD line crosses above the Signal line.
**Sell Signal (Bearish)**: The MACD line crosses below the Signal line.
**Divergence (The warning sign):** If the stock price is going up, but the MACD lines are going down (or vice versa), it means the trend is losing steam and might reverse soon.
### Rapid Rises or Falls:
If the MACD line suddenly spikes way up or drops way down away from the baseline, the stock is likely pushed too far ("overbought" or "oversold") and will soon snap back to normal.
## Why MACD is not a perfect indicator?
MACD is a lagging indicator, meaning it uses past data. It tells we what just happened, not a guaranteed prediction of what will happen.
## Beware of False Positives:
If the market is moving sideways (just bouncing up and down slightly in a tight range), MACD will cross over itself constantly, giving we a bunch of fake buy and sell signals.
## Always Use a Backup:
Smart traders rarely use MACD all by itself. They confirm its signals with other tools to make sure a trend is actually real before putting their money on the line.
## What is RSI?
RSI (Relative Strength Index) is essentially a momentum speedometer for a stock. Instead of just telling we the direction of the trend, it tells we how fast the price is moving and whether the move has gone too far, too fast.It is plotted as a single line graph on a simple scale from 0 to 100.The Math Behind It:RSI compares the average size of a stock's recent gains against its recent losses (usually over the last 14 days). If a stock closes higher several days in a row, the RSI shoots up. If it bleeds out for two weeks, the RSI sinks.
### The Magic Numbers: 30 and 70
Traders use RSI to spot when a stock is stretched like a rubber band and is due to snap back to reality. They focus on two key zones:
**Over 70 (Overbought)**: The stock has gone up too fast. Buyers might be exhausted, and a price drop or pullback could be coming (a potential sell signal).
**Under 30 (Oversold)**: The stock has dropped too fast. Sellers might be tapped out, and the price could be ready to bounce back up (a potential buy signal).
## How Traders Actually Use It?
While 30 and 70 are the standard triggers, smart traders look a bit deeper:
Adjusting for Trends: In a massive bull market (uptrend), a stock might never drop all the way to 30. Traders might consider an RSI of 40 or 50 as the "oversold" buying opportunity. Conversely, in a strong downtrend, an RSI of 50 or 60 might act as the "overbought" ceiling.
## Divergence (The Warning Sign):
Just like with MACD, if the stock price is hitting new highs but the RSI is making lower highs, the upward momentum is quietly dying. A reversal is likely coming.
Swing Rejections: This is a pattern where the RSI drops below 30, climbs back above 30, dips again (but stays above 30 this time), and then breaks out higher. It is seen as a strong confirmation that the bottom is in.
## Why is RSI not a perfect indicator?
Trends Can Break the Rules: In a blazing hot market, a stock's RSI can sit above 70 for months. If we sell just because it hit 71, we might miss out on massive gains because the price can keep climbing while the RSI stays "overbought."
**Best in "Sideways" Markets**: RSI is most reliable when a stock is just bouncing up and down in a predictable trading range. In a violent, one-way trend, it will give we false signals.
## What is Stock Volume?
Volume is simply the total number of shares that changed hands between buyers and sellers during a specific time period (usually a single trading day).

**Why Volume is our Lie Detector?**:
Traders love volume because it reveals the true market sentiment. Anyone can buy a few shares and make a stock's price tick upward, but it takes massive institutional money to create high volume.

## High Volume = Strong Interest: A price move on high volume is a strong signal. The market is aggressively participating.

## Low Volume = Weak Interest: A price move on low volume is suspicious. There isn't much power behind the move, meaning it could easily reverse.
## How to Trade Using Volume
Here is how traders practically apply this information to make decisions:

**Confirming Breakouts (The "Real Deal"):**
If a stock finally pushes past a price it has been stuck under for months, check the volume. If volume is high, the breakout is likely legitimate and will continue. If volume is low, it's probably a "fakeout" and the price will drop back down.

**If a stock's price is climbing higher and higher, but the daily volume is slowly shrinking, watch out. That means fewer and fewer buyers are willing to pay those high prices. The trend is losing steam and a reversal is likely imminent.**

Timing Entries and Exits: Smart traders wait to buy until they see high volume supporting an upward trend, and they get ready to sell or take profits when volume starts drying up.

## Common Mistakes
Panicking over a one-day spike: Sometimes volume spikes for a weird, temporary reason (like a news rumor or a major fund rebalancing its portfolio). Always look at the bigger picture before reacting to a single day.

Trusting low-volume price moves: Don't get excited if a penny stock or a highly illiquid stock jumps 10% on a random Tuesday. If the volume is tiny, that price change isn't sustainable.

Ignoring everything else: Volume is a fantastic tool, but it shouldn't be used in isolation. It works best when combined with price patterns and other indicators like the RSI and MACD we looked at earlier!
